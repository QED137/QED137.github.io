From Data to Decisions: A Beginner’s Guide to Statistical Inference

By: Janmajay Kumar

Real-World Questions: Needing Statistical Inference

Statistical inference helps determine if observed differences or trends are meaningful or just due to chance.

Examples:

  • Social Science: Do people with a college degree earn more than those without one?
  • Business: Do happier customers spend more money?
  • Finance: Is the stock market more volatile during economic downturns?
  • Physics: Does gravity vary in different locations on Earth?
  • Biology: Is a new drug better for lowering blood pressure?
  • Sports: Do athletes who practice more perform better?

Introduction: What is Statistical Inference?

Statistical inference is the process of using sample data to make generalizations about a larger population.

Example 1: Estimating household income using a sample of 500 households.

Example 2: Testing the effectiveness of a new drug using a clinical trial.

Key Statistical Concepts

  • Population Mean: The average value of a characteristic in an entire population.
  • Proportion: The percentage of a sample with a particular characteristic.
  • Standard Deviation: Measures the spread of data points around the mean.

How to Infer?

We use sampling, the Central Limit Theorem, and hypothesis testing to make inferences.

Common statistical tests: t-tests, chi-square tests, and ANOVA.

How Confident Are We About Our Inference?

Confidence intervals measure the reliability of estimates.

Hypothesis testing involves Type I and Type II errors.

Common Pitfalls

  • Confusing correlation with causation.
  • Misunderstanding the p-value.
  • Using small sample sizes, leading to unreliable results.
  • Ignoring assumptions of statistical tests.

Steps from Data to Decision

  1. Define the problem and research question.
  2. Collect a representative sample.
  3. Summarize and explore data.
  4. Formulate hypotheses.
  5. Choose the correct statistical test.
  6. Check assumptions.
  7. Compute test statistic and p-value.
  8. Make a decision based on results.

Example Problem: Weight Loss Program

Hypothesis:

\( H_0: \mu_{new} = \mu_{current} \)
\( H_1: \mu_{new} > \mu_{current} \)

Results: t-value = 3.58, p-value = 0.0004 (statistically significant).

Conclusion: The new weight loss program is more effective.

Example Problem: Two-Population Testing (Independent t-test)

Comparing two teaching methods:

\( H_0: \mu_A = \mu_B \)
\( H_1: \mu_A > \mu_B \)

t-value = 3.51, p-value < 0.05 → Method A is more effective.

Example Problem: Chi-Square Test for Independence

Is exercise related to sleep quality?

\( H_0: \) No relationship between exercise and sleep quality.
\( H_1: \) Exercise and sleep quality are related.

\( \chi^2 = 16.66, p < 0.05 \) → There is a relationship.

Example Problem: One-Way ANOVA

Comparing different fertilizers for plant growth.

\( H_0: \mu_A = \mu_B = \mu_C \)
\( H_1: \) At least one fertilizer is different.

F-value = 28, p-value < 0.05 → Significant difference in fertilizers.
